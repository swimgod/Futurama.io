Radar

Radar

Definition:

Radar stands for Radio Detection and Ranging.

How Radar Works:

A radar instrument shoots out radio waves to detect objects and determine their range, angle and velocity. The radar instrument consists of a transmitter producing high frequency radio waves and a receiver and processor that generates information about surrounding objects. In theory, Radar and LiDAR work in a similar way, but they use different signals.

Characteristics of Radar:

1. Active Sensing: Radar is an active sensing system, which is in contrast to a passive sensing system. This is because it emits radio waves and detects the reflected waves.

2. High Range Capability: Radar can achieve ranges of ~300 meters.

3. Operates in varying conditions: Radar can operate in cloudy weather conditions and in night conditions. This is because radio waves have less absorption compared to laser beams. However, poor weather conditions will still decrease the operating range.

4. Low Resolution: Radar does not provide a precise image due to longer wavelength. As a result, it is less effective at detecting small objects because surrounding objects may not reflect enough energy to be detected by the radio waves.

5. Limited Field of Vision: Generally radar only has a field of vision in the 10â€™s of degrees.

6. Computationally Light: Radar generates less data than other sensors, decreasing the heavy computational burden required for autonomous driving.

Types of Radars and Operating Ranges

There are 3 types of radar sensors, that can be classified per their operating distance ranges: Short Range Radar (SRR) 0.2 to 30m range, Medium Range Radar (MRR) in the 30-80m range and Long Range Radar (LRR) 80m up to 300m range.
